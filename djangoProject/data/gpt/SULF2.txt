### Function
Human SULF2 is an extracellular sulfatase enzyme that specifically removes 6-O-sulfate groups from heparan sulfate proteoglycans (HSPGs) on the cell surface and in the extracellular matrix. By altering the sulfation pattern of HSPGs, SULF2 modulates the interaction between heparan sulfate chains and various signaling molecules, including growth factors, chemokines, and morphogens. This regulation influences critical cellular processes such as cell signaling, proliferation, differentiation, and tissue remodeling.

### Reaction Pathways
SULF2 operates in the modification of the extracellular matrix by cleaving 6-O-sulfate groups from heparan sulfate chains. This desulfation process significantly impacts the binding affinity of heparan sulfate for key signaling molecules like fibroblast growth factors (FGFs), Wnt proteins, and Hedgehog proteins. By modulating these interactions, SULF2 influences several signaling pathways, including those involved in development, angiogenesis, and tumorigenesis. The enzyme’s activity is crucial for maintaining a balanced extracellular environment that supports normal cellular functions.

### Location
SULF2 is primarily localized on the cell surface and within the extracellular matrix. After being secreted, it remains associated with the cell membrane or extracellular matrix, where it can modify heparan sulfate chains in close proximity to signaling receptors. This localization is essential for SULF2’s role in regulating the availability and activity of signaling molecules within the local microenvironment, thereby influencing a variety of cellular responses.

### Diseases
Alterations in SULF2 expression or activity are associated with various diseases, particularly cancer. Unlike SULF1, which often acts as a tumor suppressor, SULF2 has been found to promote tumor growth in certain contexts, such as in lung cancer, glioblastoma, and hepatocellular carcinoma. Overexpression of SULF2 can enhance growth factor signaling, contributing to increased tumor proliferation, invasion, and angiogenesis. Additionally, SULF2 may play roles in other pathological conditions, including fibrosis and chronic inflammation, where dysregulated signaling leads to abnormal tissue remodeling. Understanding the function of SULF2 is important for developing therapeutic strategies that target its activity in these diseases.
