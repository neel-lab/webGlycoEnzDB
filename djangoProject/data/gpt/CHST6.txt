### Function

Human CHST6 (Carbohydrate Sulfotransferase 6), also known as keratan sulfate galactose 6-O-sulfotransferase (KSGal6ST), is an enzyme that catalyzes the transfer of sulfate groups from the donor molecule 3'-phosphoadenosine-5'-phosphosulfate (PAPS) to the 6-position of N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) residues in keratan sulfate glycosaminoglycans (KS-GAGs). This sulfation process is essential for the proper formation and function of keratan sulfate, which is involved in various cellular activities, including cell signaling, adhesion, and the maintenance of tissue integrity.

### Reaction Pathways

CHST6 operates within the biosynthesis pathway of keratan sulfate. In the Golgi apparatus, CHST6 transfers sulfate groups to the 6-position of GlcNAc residues in keratan sulfate chains. This sulfation step is crucial for generating highly sulfated keratan sulfate, which enhances its ability to interact with other molecules and fulfill its biological roles. ### Location

CHST6 is localized in the Golgi apparatus, functioning as a type II transmembrane protein. Its catalytic domain faces the lumen of the Golgi, where it can access and modify its substrates effectively. ### Diseases

Mutations or dysregulation of CHST6 are associated with macular corneal dystrophy (MCD), a genetic disorder characterized by the accumulation of improperly sulfated keratan sulfate in the cornea, leading to vision impairment. MCD is divided into two types: MCD type I, caused by mutations in the coding region of CHST6, and MCD type II, caused by mutations in the upstream regulatory region of CHST6, leading to reduced expression of the enzyme specifically in the cornea.pdf】. These defects in CHST6 result in inadequate sulfation of keratan sulfate, compromising its structural and functional properties and ultimately affecting corneal transparency and vision.

Its activity and regulation are crucial for maintaining normal physiological processes, and its dysregulation can lead to significant pathological conditions such as macular corneal dystrophy.
