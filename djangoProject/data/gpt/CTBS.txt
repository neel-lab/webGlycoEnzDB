### Function

Human CTBS (chitobiase, also known as di-N-acetylchitobiase) is an enzyme involved in the catabolic pathway of glycoproteins, specifically in the degradation of N-linked glycans. CTBS functions by cleaving the di-N-acetylchitobiose moiety from the reducing end of N-glycans, a crucial step in the degradation of glycoproteins within the lysosome. This activity helps in the recycling of glycoproteins and maintains cellular homeostasis.

### Reaction Pathways

CTBS plays a role in the lysosomal degradation pathway of glycoproteins. This pathway involves the hydrolysis of N-linked glycans, which are attached to proteins via asparagine residues. The enzyme specifically targets the N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) residues, breaking the bond between GlcNAc and the core N-glycan structure. This enzymatic action is essential for the stepwise degradation of complex glycans into simpler forms, which can then be further broken down or utilized by the cell.

### Location

CTBS is localized within the lysosome, a membrane-bound organelle responsible for the breakdown of various biomolecules. The acidic environment of the lysosome is optimal for the catalytic activity of CTBS. This localization ensures that glycoprotein degradation occurs in a controlled manner, preventing unnecessary breakdown of cellular components outside the lysosome.

Deficiencies or malfunctions in CTBS can lead to severe metabolic disorders. For instance, impaired CTBS activity is linked to lysosomal storage diseases, where undegraded glycoproteins accumulate within lysosomes, disrupting cellular function. Such accumulation can lead to a variety of symptoms, including developmental delays, neurological deficits, and other systemic issues. Although specific diseases directly attributed to CTBS deficiency are rare, its role in the broader context of lysosomal storage disorders underscores its importance in cellular metabolism and health.
