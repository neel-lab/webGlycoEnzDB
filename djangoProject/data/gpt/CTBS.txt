Human CTBS, known as Cystatin B, is an enzyme inhibitor with a significant role in cellular protease regulation. It's particularly involved in the inhibition of lysosomal cysteine proteases, which are crucial for protein degradation and turnover.

Function and Reaction Pathways:
CTBS functions as a potent inhibitor of cathepsins, a group of lysosomal cysteine proteases. These proteases are involved in the breakdown of proteins within lysosomes, a process vital for cellular homeostasis and the recycling of cellular components. By inhibiting cathepsins, CTBS regulates protein degradation, ensuring that this process does not become overactive, which could lead to cellular damage. Its role is particularly significant in preventing the uncontrolled breakdown of myelin proteins in the central nervous system.

Location:
CTBS is primarily located in lysosomes, the organelles responsible for waste processing and recycling in cells. Its presence in lysosomes is essential for the direct regulation of protease activity, allowing for efficient and controlled protein degradation.

Diseases:
Mutations in the CTBS gene are linked to a rare genetic disorder known as Unverricht-Lundborg disease (EPM1), a type of progressive myoclonus epilepsy. This condition is characterized by seizures, muscle twitching (myoclonus), and neurological deterioration. The loss of CTBS function leads to the accumulation of misfolded proteins and the subsequent degeneration of neurons, particularly affecting the cerebellum and leading to the symptoms observed in EPM1.
