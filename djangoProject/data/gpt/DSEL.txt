### Function

Human DSEL (dermatan sulfate epimerase-like, also known as DS-epi2) is an enzyme that plays a critical role in the biosynthesis of dermatan sulfate (DS), a type of glycosaminoglycan. DSEL functions by catalyzing the epimerization of D-glucuronic acid (GlcA) to L-iduronic acid (IdoA) within the dermatan sulfate chains. This conversion is essential for the formation of functional DS, which contributes to the structural integrity and various biological activities of the extracellular matrix.

### Reaction Pathways

DSEL operates in the dermatan sulfate biosynthesis pathway, where it specifically catalyzes the epimerization of GlcA to IdoA during the elongation phase of glycosaminoglycan chains in the Golgi apparatus. This enzymatic activity introduces conformational flexibility into the DS chains, allowing them to interact with a variety of proteins, including growth factors, cytokines, and structural proteins, thereby modulating cellular processes such as adhesion, migration, and proliferation.

### Location

DSEL is localized in the Golgi apparatus, the cellular organelle responsible for the modification, sorting, and packaging of proteins and lipids. Within the Golgi, DSEL is strategically positioned to interact with glycosaminoglycan chains as they are being synthesized. This localization ensures that DSEL can effectively catalyze the necessary epimerization of GlcA to IdoA, facilitating the proper formation and function of dermatan sulfate.

### Diseases

Mutations or deficiencies in the DSEL gene can lead to disorders associated with defective dermatan sulfate biosynthesis. Although no specific diseases have been directly attributed to DSEL mutations, the enzyme's role in DS formation suggests that such mutations could contribute to conditions like Ehlers-Danlos syndrome musculocontractural type (EDSMC). This disorder is characterized by symptoms such as skin hyperextensibility, joint hypermobility, and other connective tissue abnormalities, resulting from impaired DS biosynthesis and function. Further research is needed to fully elucidate the impact of DSEL deficiencies on human health.
