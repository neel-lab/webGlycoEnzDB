HK3 shares functional similarities with its family members, HK1 and HK2, but it possesses unique kinetic properties and tissue-specific expression patterns. This distinct expression pattern suggests a specialized role for HK3 in the metabolic regulation of these organs.

While its direct involvement in diseases is less characterized compared to HK1 and HK2, any dysregulation in HK3 activity could potentially contribute to metabolic disorders. For example, in diabetes, impaired glucose metabolism due to dysfunctional hexokinase activity can lead to elevated blood glucose levels and subsequent complications.

In cancer biology, the role of HK3 is less defined than HK2, which is heavily implicated in the Warburg effect. However, given the fundamental role of hexokinases in cellular metabolism, it is conceivable that HK3 could play a role in cancer cells under certain conditions, possibly contributing to altered metabolic states.
