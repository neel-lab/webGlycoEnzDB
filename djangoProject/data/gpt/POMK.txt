### Function
Human POMK, or protein-O-mannose kinase, is an enzyme that plays a critical role in the glycosylation of alpha-dystroglycan, a protein essential for the structural integrity of muscle cells and the proper functioning of the nervous system. POMK specifically phosphorylates the mannose residue on O-mannosyl glycan chains attached to alpha-dystroglycan. This phosphorylation is a key step in the maturation of alpha-dystroglycan, enabling it to bind to extracellular matrix proteins like laminin, which is crucial for maintaining the stability and function of muscle and nerve cells.

### Reaction Pathways
POMK functions within the O-mannosylation glycosylation pathway. In this pathway, POMK catalyzes the transfer of a phosphate group to the mannose residue on O-mannosyl glycan chains of alpha-dystroglycan. This phosphorylation is essential for the subsequent addition of other sugars and modifications that create a fully functional glycan structure. Properly modified alpha-dystroglycan is necessary for binding to extracellular matrix components, ensuring the integrity of muscle tissues and the correct development of the brain.

### Location
POMK is localized in the Golgi apparatus, where it participates in the post-translational modification of proteins. The Golgi is the central hub for processing and modifying proteins before they are transported to their final destinations. The presence of POMK in the Golgi ensures that alpha-dystroglycan is correctly phosphorylated and prepared for further glycosylation steps, allowing it to function properly when it reaches the cell membrane.

### Diseases
Mutations in the POMK gene are associated with congenital muscular dystrophies, such as Walker-Warburg syndrome (WWS) and muscle-eye-brain disease (MEB). These conditions are characterized by severe muscle weakness, brain malformations, and eye abnormalities. The diseases arise because defective POMK activity leads to insufficient phosphorylation of alpha-dystroglycan, resulting in improper glycosylation and a failure to bind to extracellular matrix proteins. This disruption in cell-matrix interactions causes the structural and functional defects observed in these disorders.
