### Function
Human MAN1C1, or alpha-1,2-mannosidase 1C1, is an enzyme involved in the processing and maturation of N-glycans, which are essential for proper protein folding and function. MAN1C1 specifically catalyzes the removal of alpha-1,2-linked mannose residues from high-mannose oligosaccharides on newly synthesized glycoproteins. This trimming step is crucial for the conversion of these high-mannose glycans into complex or hybrid N-glycans, which play vital roles in various cellular processes, including protein stability, cell-cell communication, and immune recognition.

### Reaction Pathways
MAN1C1 operates within the N-glycosylation pathway, a crucial post-translational modification process. After glycoproteins are synthesized and initially glycosylated in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), they are transported to the Golgi apparatus, where MAN1C1 further trims mannose residues. This trimming is a key step in the processing of N-glycans, facilitating their maturation into complex structures that are critical for proper protein function. The activity of MAN1C1 is essential for ensuring that glycoproteins are correctly processed and functional as they move through the secretory pathway.

### Location
MAN1C1 is localized in the Golgi apparatus, particularly in the medial-Golgi compartment. The Golgi is the central organelle for modifying, sorting, and packaging proteins and lipids. MAN1C1’s presence in the Golgi enables it to perform its enzymatic function in a critical location where glycoproteins are processed before they are sent to their final destinations within the cell or secreted outside of it.

### Diseases
While specific diseases directly caused by mutations in the MAN1C1 gene are not well-documented, defects in N-glycosylation enzymes like MAN1C1 can lead to a group of inherited conditions known as congenital disorders of glycosylation (CDGs). These disorders result in a wide range of symptoms, including developmental delays, immune deficiencies, and neurological problems, due to improper glycosylation of proteins. Additionally, abnormal glycosylation patterns, which could involve enzymes like MAN1C1, have been implicated in various cancers and other diseases, where they can affect cell signaling, adhesion, and immune evasion.
