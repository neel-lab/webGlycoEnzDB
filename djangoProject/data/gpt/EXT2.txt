Human EXT2 (Exostosin Glycosyltransferase 2) is a key enzyme in the biosynthesis of heparan sulfate, a vital glycosaminoglycan with numerous roles in cell signaling, development, and homeostasis. EXT2 works in tandem with EXT1 to facilitate the elongation of the heparan sulfate chain, a process essential for the formation of functional heparan sulfate proteoglycans.

Function and Reaction Pathways:
EXT2's primary function is to catalyze the addition of sugar units to the growing heparan sulfate chain. Specifically, it alternately adds glucuronic acid and N-acetylglucosamine residues, a process critical for generating the diverse structures and sizes of heparan sulfate chains. These modifications are crucial as they determine the ability of heparan sulfate to interact with various proteins, such as growth factors, enzymes, and structural proteins, influencing a myriad of biological processes, including cell proliferation, differentiation, and migration.

Location:
EXT2 is located in the Golgi apparatus, the cellular site where glycosaminoglycans are synthesized and modified. This localization is strategic for its role in the sequential assembly of heparan sulfate chains.

Diseases:
Mutations in the EXT2 gene are associated with hereditary multiple exostoses (HME), a condition characterized by the development of multiple benign bone tumors called osteochondromas. These growths can lead to skeletal deformities, restricted movement, and, in some cases, may progress to malignant chondrosarcoma. The molecular mechanism linking EXT2 mutations to osteochondroma formation is thought to involve disrupted heparan sulfate biosynthesis, affecting cell signaling pathways that regulate bone growth and differentiation.
