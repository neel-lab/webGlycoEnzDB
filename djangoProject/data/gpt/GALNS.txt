### Function
Human GALNS (N-acetylgalactosamine-6-sulfatase) is a lysosomal enzyme responsible for breaking down glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), specifically keratan sulfate and chondroitin-6-sulfate. GALNS hydrolyzes the sulfate groups from the 6-position of N-acetylgalactosamine residues in these GAGs. This desulfation step is crucial for the subsequent degradation of GAGs within the lysosome, preventing their accumulation and maintaining normal cellular function.

### Reaction Pathways
GALNS functions in the lysosomal degradation pathway, specifically targeting keratan sulfate and chondroitin-6-sulfate. These GAGs are complex molecules found in the extracellular matrix and on cell surfaces. The enzyme removes the sulfate groups from N-acetylgalactosamine residues, which is a necessary step before other enzymes can further break down the GAGs into their constituent sugars for recycling or excretion.

### Location
GALNS is localized in the lysosomes, the organelles responsible for the breakdown and recycling of various biomolecules. Within the lysosome, GALNS acts on its GAG substrates, ensuring their proper degradation. The enzyme's activity in this location is vital for preventing the buildup of partially degraded GAGs, which can lead to cellular dysfunction.

### Diseases
Mutations in the GALNS gene lead to the lysosomal storage disorder mucopolysaccharidosis type IVA (MPS IVA), also known as Morquio A syndrome. This autosomal recessive condition is characterized by the accumulation of keratan sulfate and chondroitin-6-sulfate in tissues, leading to severe skeletal abnormalities, growth retardation, and other systemic complications. The disease manifests in childhood and progresses over time, often resulting in significant morbidity. Enzyme replacement therapy and other treatments are used to manage the symptoms, but no cure currently exists.
