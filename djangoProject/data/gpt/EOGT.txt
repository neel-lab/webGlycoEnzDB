Human EOGT (EGF Domain-Specific O-Linked N-Acetylglucosamine Transferase) is a unique enzyme playing a crucial role in the post-translational modification of proteins, specifically in the addition of O-GlcNAc (O-linked N-acetylglucosamine) to serine or threonine residues. This modification occurs predominantly in epidermal growth factor-like (EGF-like) repeats, which are found in a variety of proteins implicated in cell signaling and development.

Function and Reaction Pathways:
EOGT catalyzes the O-GlcNAcylation of proteins containing EGF-like repeats. This modification is important for protein-protein interactions and signaling pathways, as it influences the structure and function of the modified proteins. O-GlcNAcylation by EOGT is particularly significant in Notch signaling, a pathway crucial for cell differentiation, proliferation, and apoptosis. The proper functioning of Notch signaling is essential for various developmental processes and the maintenance of stem cells.

Location:
EOGT is located in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where it interacts with newly synthesized proteins destined for secretion or membrane localization. This location allows EOGT to modify proteins early in their biosynthesis, impacting their subsequent folding, trafficking, and function.

Diseases:
Dysfunction in EOGT has been linked to Adams-Oliver syndrome, a rare developmental disorder characterized by defects in skin, skull, and limb development. Mutations in the EOGT gene can disrupt normal O-GlcNAcylation, leading to abnormalities in Notch signaling and other developmental pathways. Additionally, given the importance of Notch signaling in various physiological processes, aberrations in EOGT activity could potentially contribute to other developmental disorders, certain cancers, and neurodegenerative diseases.
