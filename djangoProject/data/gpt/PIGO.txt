Human PIGO (Phosphatidylinositol Glycan Anchor Biosynthesis Class O) is a crucial enzyme in the biosynthesis of glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchors. GPI anchors are complex glycolipids that attach a wide array of proteins to the cell surface, playing essential roles in various cellular processes, including signal transduction, cell adhesion, and immune responses.

PIGO is specifically responsible for the addition of phosphoethanolamine (EtNP) to the inositol ring of phosphatidylinositol, a late and critical step in the GPI anchor biosynthetic pathway. This modification is essential for the proper anchoring of proteins to the cell membrane via GPI anchors.

Located in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), PIGO works in conjunction with other enzymes involved in the GPI anchor biosynthesis pathway. The proper function of GPI-anchored proteins on the cell surface, including enzymes, receptors, and adhesion molecules, is dependent on the successful biosynthesis of GPI anchors, wherein PIGO plays a key role.

Dysfunction in PIGO, due to mutations or deficiencies, can lead to GPI anchor deficiencies, resulting in a group of rare genetic disorders collectively known as GPI biosynthesis defects (GPIBDs). Patients with GPIBDs can present a range of symptoms, including intellectual disability, seizures, and developmental delays, highlighting the critical role of GPI anchors in neurological development and function.
