### Function
Human HPSE, or heparanase, is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in the degradation of heparan sulfate, a key component of the extracellular matrix (ECM) and basement membranes. HPSE specifically cleaves heparan sulfate chains, leading to the remodeling of the ECM and the release of bioactive molecules such as growth factors and cytokines. This activity is essential for processes like tissue repair, angiogenesis, and cell migration.

### Reaction Pathways
HPSE functions within the extracellular matrix remodeling pathway. By cleaving heparan sulfate, HPSE facilitates the breakdown of the ECM, which is necessary for cell movement and invasion during processes such as wound healing, inflammation, and cancer metastasis. The cleavage of heparan sulfate by HPSE also liberates growth factors and other signaling molecules sequestered in the ECM, which can then interact with cell surface receptors to initiate signaling pathways that promote cell proliferation, differentiation, and survival.

### Location
HPSE is synthesized as an inactive precursor and is primarily located in lysosomes, where it undergoes proteolytic processing to become an active enzyme. Once activated, HPSE can be secreted or released into the extracellular space, where it acts on heparan sulfate chains in the ECM and basement membranes. The enzyme's ability to modulate the ECM makes it a key player in processes that require ECM degradation and remodeling.

### Diseases
Overexpression or dysregulation of HPSE is associated with various pathological conditions, particularly cancer and inflammatory diseases. In cancer, elevated levels of HPSE contribute to tumor progression by enhancing ECM degradation, facilitating tumor cell invasion, and promoting angiogenesis, which is the formation of new blood vessels that supply the tumor. HPSE is also involved in the metastasis of cancer cells to distant organs. Additionally, HPSE has been implicated in chronic inflammatory diseases, such as rheumatoid arthritis, where its activity contributes to tissue destruction and inflammation. Due to its significant role in disease processes, HPSE is a potential therapeutic target for cancer and other conditions characterized by excessive ECM remodeling.
