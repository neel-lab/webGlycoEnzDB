### Function
Human MOGS, or mannosyl-oligosaccharide glucosidase, is a critical enzyme involved in the early stages of N-glycosylation, a process essential for proper protein folding and function. MOGS catalyzes the hydrolysis of the terminal glucose residues from the precursor glycan structure attached to nascent glycoproteins in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). This trimming step is crucial for the subsequent processing and maturation of glycoproteins, ensuring their correct folding and eventual function within the cell.

### Reaction Pathways
MOGS operates within the N-glycosylation pathway, specifically in the trimming of the glucose residues from the Glc3Man9GlcNAc2 oligosaccharide. After the addition of this oligosaccharide to the nascent protein in the ER, MOGS removes the outermost glucose residues, which is a prerequisite for further processing by other glycosidases and for the glycoprotein to interact with chaperone proteins involved in quality control. This step is essential for the proper folding of glycoproteins and their subsequent transport to the Golgi apparatus for further modification.

### Location
MOGS is localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), the cellular organelle responsible for the synthesis and initial modification of proteins. The ER provides the necessary environment for MOGS to carry out its function in the early stages of glycoprotein maturation. By trimming the glucose residues in the ER, MOGS ensures that glycoproteins are properly folded and prepared for further processing as they move through the secretory pathway.

### Diseases
Mutations in the MOGS gene can lead to a congenital disorder known as congenital disorder of glycosylation type IIb (CDG-IIb). This rare genetic condition is characterized by defective glycoprotein processing due to the inability to properly trim glucose residues from glycoproteins, leading to the accumulation of misfolded proteins. Symptoms of CDG-IIb can include developmental delays, neurological abnormalities, immune deficiencies, and other systemic issues.
