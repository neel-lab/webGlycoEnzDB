Human GALE, or UDP-galactose-4-epimerase, is a critical enzyme in carbohydrate metabolism. It is responsible for the interconversion of UDP-galactose to UDP-glucose, a key step in the Leloir pathway of galactose metabolism. This enzyme ensures the balance between these two nucleotide sugars, which are vital for various biosynthetic processes, including the synthesis of glycoproteins and glycolipids.

Structurally, human GALE is a homodimeric protein, with each monomer having a molecular mass of 38 kDa and consisting of 348 amino acids. This structure is consistent with other GALE proteins across different species, although some biochemical properties may vary. The enzyme generally falls into one of three groups based on the type of substrates it acts on: non-acetylated substrates, both non-acetylated and acetylated substrates, and acetylated substrates​​.

Mutations in the GALE gene can lead to a condition known as type III galactosemia. This disorder is characterized by an impaired ability to convert galactose to glucose, leading to the accumulation of galactose and its derivatives in the body. Symptoms can range from mild to severe and may include cataracts, delayed growth, learning disabilities, and in severe cases, neurological impairments. The severity of the condition is typically related to the extent to which the mutation affects the structure, cofactor binding, substrate binding, and overall enzymatic activity of GALE​​.
