RPN1, located in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), is integral to the transfer of pre-assembled oligosaccharides onto nascent polypeptides, a process crucial for proper protein folding and stability.

RPN1's primary function is to facilitate the N-glycosylation of newly synthesized proteins. This process involves the attachment of an oligosaccharide to an asparagine residue in the consensus sequence Asn-X-Ser/Thr (where X can be any amino acid except proline) of a polypeptide chain. N-glycosylation, occurring co-translationally in the ER, is essential for protein folding, quality control, trafficking, and degradation.

The OST complex, of which RPN1 is a part, is responsible for scanning the nascent polypeptides and adding the glycan moiety at the appropriate sites. RPN1 not only contributes to the catalytic activity of the complex but also to the recognition of glycosylation sites on the substrates.

Disruptions or mutations in RPN1 can lead to abnormalities in protein glycosylation, affecting protein function and stability. While specific diseases directly linked to RPN1 mutations are not widely documented, the significance of N-glycosylation in cellular function implies that such disruptions could contribute to various pathologies, particularly those related to protein misfolding and ER stress.
