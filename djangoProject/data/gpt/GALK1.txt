Human GALK1 (Galactokinase 1) is an essential enzyme in the metabolism of galactose, a simple sugar that plays a significant role in various biological processes. GALK1 catalyzes the first step in the Leloir pathway, crucial for the efficient utilization of galactose derived from dietary sources.

Function and Reaction Pathways:
GALK1 phosphorylates galactose to form galactose-1-phosphate, utilizing ATP as a phosphate donor. This reaction is vital for the subsequent steps in the Leloir pathway, which converts galactose into glucose-1-phosphate, eventually leading to its incorporation into the glycolysis pathway. The effective conversion of galactose into glucose derivatives is critical for energy production and the synthesis of glycoproteins and glycolipids.

Location:
GALK1 is predominantly found in the cytoplasm, where it accesses galactose that has been transported into the cell. Its cytoplasmic localization facilitates its role in the initial metabolism of galactose, preparing it for further processing in the Leloir pathway.

Diseases:
Deficiency in GALK1 activity is linked to galactokinase deficiency, a type of galactosemia. This genetic disorder is characterized by an inability to properly metabolize galactose, leading to the accumulation of galactose and galactitol (a reduced form of galactose) in the blood and tissues. Symptoms of galactokinase deficiency can include cataracts, delayed growth, and developmental problems, although it is generally milder than classic galactosemia, which is caused by galactose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase (GALT) deficiency.
