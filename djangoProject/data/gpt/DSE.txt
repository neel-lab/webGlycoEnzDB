### Function

Human DSE (dermatan sulfate epimerase), also known as DS-epi1, is an enzyme responsible for converting D-glucuronic acid (GlcA) to L-iduronic acid (IdoA) within dermatan sulfate (DS) chains. This epimerization process is essential for the biosynthesis of dermatan sulfate, a glycosaminoglycan involved in the structural integrity of the extracellular matrix and various cellular functions. The presence of IdoA residues in DS chains contributes to their ability to interact with proteins and modulate biological activities such as cell adhesion, migration, and proliferation.

### Reaction Pathways

DSE operates in the dermatan sulfate biosynthesis pathway, specifically catalyzing the epimerization of GlcA to IdoA. This modification occurs during the elongation phase of glycosaminoglycan chains in the Golgi apparatus. The formation of IdoA residues by DSE is critical for the functional diversity of dermatan sulfate. This epimerization process allows DS to bind to various growth factors, cytokines, and extracellular matrix proteins, influencing numerous physiological processes and contributing to tissue repair and development.

### Location

DSE is localized in the Golgi apparatus, where it acts on nascent glycosaminoglycan chains during their biosynthesis. The Golgi apparatus is the cellular organelle responsible for the modification, sorting, and packaging of proteins and lipids. DSE’s localization within the Golgi ensures that it can effectively interact with and modify glycosaminoglycan chains as they are being synthesized, ensuring the proper formation of functional dermatan sulfate.

### Diseases

Mutations or deficiencies in the DSE gene can lead to disorders related to defective dermatan sulfate biosynthesis. One notable condition is the Ehlers-Danlos syndrome musculocontractural type (EDSMC), which is characterized by symptoms such as skin hyperextensibility, joint hypermobility, and other connective tissue abnormalities. Patients with EDSMC often exhibit mutations in genes related to dermatan sulfate biosynthesis, including DSE.
