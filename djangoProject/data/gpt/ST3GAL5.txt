### Function
Human ST3GAL5, also known as GM3 synthase, is an enzyme responsible for the synthesis of ganglioside GM3, a critical component of cell membranes, particularly in the nervous system. It catalyzes the transfer of sialic acid to lactosylceramide (LacCer) to produce GM3, the simplest ganglioside. GM3 serves as a precursor for the synthesis of more complex gangliosides, which are essential for cell-cell communication, signal transduction, and the modulation of cell growth.

### Reaction Pathways
ST3GAL5 functions in the ganglioside biosynthesis pathway. It transfers sialic acid from CMP-sialic acid to the terminal galactose of lactosylceramide, forming GM3. This reaction is a critical step in the production of gangliosides, which are glycosphingolipids involved in various cellular processes, including the modulation of signal transduction pathways, particularly in neuronal cells. GM3 and its derivatives play significant roles in cell recognition, adhesion, and signal transduction.

### Location
ST3GAL5 is localized in the Golgi apparatus, where it participates in the biosynthesis of gangliosides. The Golgi is a central organelle for the processing and modification of lipids and proteins. The presence of ST3GAL5 in the Golgi membrane ensures that GM3 is synthesized as glycosphingolipids are being processed and sorted for incorporation into the cell membrane, particularly in neurons where gangliosides are most abundant.

### Diseases
Mutations in the ST3GAL5 gene are associated with rare genetic disorders such as GM3 synthase deficiency, also known as Amish infantile epilepsy syndrome. This disorder is characterized by severe developmental delays, profound intellectual disability, and intractable epilepsy. The absence or malfunction of ST3GAL5 leads to a deficiency in GM3 and other complex gangliosides, disrupting normal brain development and function. Additionally, altered expression of ST3GAL5 has been implicated in cancer, where changes in ganglioside composition can affect cell proliferation, migration, and apoptosis. Understanding the role of ST3GAL5 is crucial for developing therapies for these conditions.
