Human OGA (O-GlcNAcase) is an enzyme crucial for the dynamic and reversible post-translational modification of proteins through O-GlcNAcylation. OGA specifically catalyzes the removal of N-acetylglucosamine (O-GlcNAc) residues from serine and threonine residues on nuclear and cytoplasmic proteins. This process is vital for regulating the O-GlcNAcylation status of proteins, a modification implicated in numerous cellular functions including signaling, transcription, and metabolism.

Located predominantly in the cytoplasm and nucleus, OGA acts in tandem with the enzyme O-GlcNAc transferase (OGT), which adds O-GlcNAc groups to proteins. The dynamic balance maintained by OGA and OGT is essential for the proper function of various cellular processes. Dysregulation in O-GlcNAcylation, due to imbalances in OGA activity, has been implicated in several diseases, particularly in neurodegenerative disorders such as Alzheimer's disease and Parkinson's disease. In these conditions, abnormal O-GlcNAcylation patterns can contribute to the misfolding and aggregation of proteins, a hallmark of these diseases.

Furthermore, alterations in O-GlcNAcase activity have been linked to diabetes and cancer, where aberrant O-GlcNAcylation levels can affect cell signaling pathways, cell cycle regulation, and metabolic processes.
