### Function
Human PIGS, or phosphatidylinositol glycan anchor biosynthesis class S, is an enzyme involved in the later stages of glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchor biosynthesis. GPI anchors are crucial for attaching certain proteins to the cell membrane, where they play important roles in cellular processes such as signal transduction, immune responses, and cell adhesion. PIGS is a component of the GPI transamidase complex, which facilitates the transfer of the GPI anchor to proteins, allowing them to be anchored to the cell membrane.

### Reaction Pathways
PIGS functions within the GPI anchor biosynthesis pathway, particularly in the GPI transamidation process. This enzyme is part of a multi-protein complex that performs the transamidation reaction, where the GPI anchor is attached to the carboxyl terminus of precursor proteins after the signal peptide is cleaved. This step is critical for ensuring that proteins are correctly anchored to the cell membrane, enabling them to perform their biological functions effectively.

### Location
PIGS is localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), the cellular organelle where protein synthesis and initial post-translational modifications occur. In the ER, PIGS works as part of the GPI transamidase complex to attach GPI anchors to proteins. This ER localization is crucial for PIGS to efficiently interact with precursor proteins and facilitate their proper attachment to the cell membrane via GPI anchors.

These disorders result from defects in GPI anchor biosynthesis, leading to the improper localization and function of GPI-anchored proteins.
