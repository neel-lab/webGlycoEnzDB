### Function

Human CALR (calreticulin) is a multifunctional protein primarily known for its role as a calcium-binding chaperone in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). It assists in the proper folding of newly synthesized glycoproteins and helps maintain calcium homeostasis within the ER. Calreticulin also plays a role in various cellular processes, including cell adhesion, gene expression, and immune response modulation. Its ability to bind calcium ions with high capacity but low affinity is crucial for regulating intracellular calcium levels and ensuring the correct folding and quality control of glycoproteins.

### Reaction Pathways

CALR is involved in several key pathways, particularly those related to protein folding and calcium signaling. Within the ER, calreticulin, along with calnexin, forms part of the calreticulin/calnexin cycle, which ensures the proper folding of N-glycosylated proteins. Calreticulin binds to monoglucosylated glycoproteins, preventing them from aggregating and promoting their correct folding. Additionally, calreticulin participates in calcium signaling pathways, where it acts as a calcium buffer and regulator. It modulates the storage and release of calcium within the ER, thereby influencing various signaling cascades and cellular functions.

### Location

Calreticulin is primarily localized in the endoplasmic reticulum, where it performs its chaperone and calcium-buffering functions. However, it is also found in other cellular compartments, including the cytoplasm and the cell surface, where it can interact with different proteins and contribute to various cellular processes. The presence of calreticulin on the cell surface, for example, has been implicated in the modulation of immune responses and cell adhesion. Its versatile localization underscores its multifaceted roles in maintaining cellular homeostasis and functionality.

### Diseases

Dysregulation or mutations in CALR are associated with several diseases. Notably, mutations in the CALR gene are linked to myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPNs), a group of blood cancers characterized by the excessive production of blood cells. These mutations often result in a gain-of-function that promotes abnormal cell proliferation and survival. Additionally, altered calreticulin levels and function have been observed in autoimmune diseases, where it can act as an autoantigen, and in various cancers, where it may influence tumor growth and immune evasion. The broad impact of calreticulin on cellular processes highlights its significance in health and disease.
