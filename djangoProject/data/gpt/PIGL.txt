Human PIGL (Phosphatidylinositol Glycan Anchor Biosynthesis Class L) is an essential enzyme in the biosynthesis of glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchors. GPI anchors are complex glycolipids that link proteins to the cell membrane, facilitating various cellular functions such as signal transduction, cell adhesion, and immune response. PIGL is specifically responsible for the de-N-acetylation of N-acetylglucosaminyl phosphatidylinositol, an early and crucial step in the GPI biosynthetic pathway.

PIGL is localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where it contributes to the initial stages of GPI anchor assembly. This process is vital for the proper attachment of GPI anchors to proteins, determining their correct localization and function at the cell surface. GPI-anchored proteins include enzymes, receptors, and adhesion molecules, playing significant roles in various biological processes.

Deficiencies or mutations in PIGL can lead to GPI anchor biosynthesis disorders. One such condition is CHIME syndrome (also known as Zunich neuroectodermal syndrome), a rare multi-systemic disorder. CHIME syndrome is characterized by colobomas, congenital heart defects, early-onset migratory ichthyosiform dermatosis, mental retardation (intellectual disability), and ear anomalies. The disorder highlights the crucial role of GPI-anchored proteins in development and various physiological processes.
