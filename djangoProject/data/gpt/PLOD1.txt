### Function
Human PLOD1, or procollagen-lysine, 2-oxoglutarate 5-dioxygenase 1, is an enzyme critical for the post-translational modification of collagen, the most abundant protein in the human body. PLOD1 specifically hydroxylates lysine residues in collagen, converting them into hydroxylysine. This modification is essential for the stability and function of collagen, as hydroxylysine residues are necessary for the cross-linking of collagen fibers and for the attachment of carbohydrate molecules, which enhance the strength and structural integrity of connective tissues.

### Reaction Pathways
PLOD1 operates in the collagen biosynthesis pathway, where it catalyzes the hydroxylation of lysine residues within the collagen triple helix. This reaction requires molecular oxygen, iron, and 2-oxoglutarate as cofactors. The hydroxylated lysine residues can then undergo further modifications, such as glycosylation, which are crucial for the proper assembly and function of collagen fibers. These fibers provide structural support to tissues like skin, bones, tendons, and blood vessels.

### Location
PLOD1 is localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where collagen synthesis and modification occur. The ER provides the optimal environment for PLOD1 to hydroxylate lysine residues in procollagen molecules before they are secreted and assembled into mature collagen fibers in the extracellular matrix. PLOD1's location in the ER is essential for its role in ensuring the proper post-translational modification of collagen.

### Diseases
Mutations in the PLOD1 gene are associated with Ehlers-Danlos syndrome type VI (EDS VI), also known as the kyphoscoliotic type. EDS VI is a rare genetic disorder characterized by joint hypermobility, skin hyperextensibility, and a tendency for easy bruising and abnormal scar formation. The disease arises due to defective hydroxylation of lysine residues in collagen, leading to weak connective tissues that are prone to damage. Understanding PLOD1's function is crucial for diagnosing EDS VI and exploring potential treatments to manage the symptoms associated with this connective tissue disorder.
