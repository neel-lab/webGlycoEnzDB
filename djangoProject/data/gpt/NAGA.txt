NAGA specifically hydrolyzes terminal alpha-N-acetylgalactosamine residues from glycopeptides and glycolipids, a crucial step in the breakdown of complex molecules into simpler ones for cellular recycling and metabolism.

NAGA is located in the lysosomes, where it plays a critical role in the degradation process of glycoconjugates. Schindler disease is characterized by the abnormal accumulation of glycoconjugates in the lysosomes, leading to a range of symptoms that can vary significantly in severity.
