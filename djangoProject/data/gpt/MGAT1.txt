MGAT1, also known as mannosyl (alpha-1,3-)-glycoprotein beta-1,2-N-acetylglucosaminyltransferase, is an essential enzyme in the process of glycosylation, specifically in the synthesis of complex N-linked glycans in human cells. It plays a crucial role in the modification of glycoproteins, which are vital for various cellular functions, including cell signaling, immune response, and protein stability.

Functionally, MGAT1 is responsible for adding N-acetylglucosamine to the trimannosyl core of N-linked glycans in glycoproteins. This step is critical in the conversion of high-mannose oligosaccharides to complex or hybrid N-linked glycans. The action of MGAT1 is vital for the structural and functional maturation of glycoproteins, influencing their stability, localization, and interactions within the cell.

MGAT1 is a type II transmembrane protein that resides in the medial Golgi apparatus, where it functions primarily as a homodimer or heterodimer. Its location in the Golgi apparatus is strategic for its role in glycoprotein processing, allowing it to modify glycoproteins as they transit through the secretory pathway​​.

Diseases associated with dysfunctions in MGAT1 or the glycosylation pathway can include congenital disorders of glycosylation, which can result in a wide range of symptoms depending on the specific glycosylation steps affected. Additionally, alterations in N-glycosylation patterns, potentially due to MGAT1 dysfunction, have been implicated in various diseases, including cancer and autoimmune disorders.
