### Function
Human GLA, or alpha-galactosidase A, is an enzyme that plays a critical role in the lysosomal degradation of glycosphingolipids. GLA specifically catalyzes the hydrolysis of terminal alpha-galactosyl residues from glycosphingolipids, such as globotriaosylceramide (Gb3), converting them into simpler molecules that can be further degraded or recycled. This process is essential for the breakdown and removal of complex lipids from cells, particularly in the kidney, heart, and nervous system.

### Reaction Pathways
GLA functions within the lysosomal degradation pathway, where it acts on glycosphingolipids like Gb3. After the terminal alpha-galactosyl residue is removed by GLA, the resulting products can be further processed by other lysosomal enzymes. This breakdown is crucial for preventing the accumulation of glycosphingolipids within cells, which could otherwise disrupt cellular function and lead to toxicity. The pathway that GLA participates in is part of the broader sphingolipid metabolism, which is vital for maintaining cellular membrane integrity and function.

### Diseases
Deficiency or malfunction of GLA leads to Fabry disease, a rare genetic disorder characterized by the accumulation of Gb3 in various tissues, including the kidneys, heart, and nervous system. This accumulation results from the inability of the defective GLA enzyme to properly degrade Gb3, leading to a range of symptoms such as pain, kidney dysfunction, heart disease, and stroke. Fabry disease is an X-linked disorder, meaning it primarily affects males, though females can also be symptomatic. Treatment for Fabry disease often involves enzyme replacement therapy to restore GLA function and reduce the buildup of glycosphingolipids in affected tissues.
