### Function

Human CHST5 (Carbohydrate Sulfotransferase 5), also known as N-acetylglucosamine-6-O-sulfotransferase, is an enzyme that catalyzes the transfer of sulfate groups from 3'-phosphoadenosine-5'-phosphosulfate (PAPS) to the 6-position of N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) residues in glycoproteins and glycosaminoglycans. ### Reaction Pathways

CHST5 operates within the Golgi apparatus, where it performs its enzymatic activity on GlcNAc residues. This reaction is a key step in the synthesis of sulfated glycosaminoglycans, particularly keratan sulfate. The enzyme transfers a sulfate group to the 6-hydroxyl position of GlcNAc, resulting in the formation of 6-sulfated GlcNAc. This modification is essential for the functional properties of keratan sulfate and other sulfated glycans, which interact with various proteins and receptors to mediate cellular processes such as adhesion, migration, and signaling.pdf】.

### Location

CHST5 is localized in the Golgi apparatus, functioning as a type II transmembrane protein. Its catalytic domain faces the lumen of the Golgi, where it has access to its substrate molecules. This localization is crucial for its role in the post-translational modification of glycoproteins and proteoglycans, ensuring these molecules are properly sulfated before being transported to their final destinations within the cell or extracellular matrix.pdf】.

### Diseases

Mutations or dysregulation of CHST5 can lead to various pathological conditions, particularly those affecting the eyes and connective tissues. Abnormal CHST5 activity has been linked to macular corneal dystrophy, a disorder characterized by progressive vision loss due to the accumulation of improperly sulfated keratan sulfate in the cornea. Additionally, altered expression of CHST5 may contribute to inflammatory diseases by affecting the sulfation patterns of glycans involved in immune responses.
