### Function
Human GANAB, or glucosidase II alpha subunit, is a key enzyme involved in the process of glycoprotein maturation. GANAB functions as part of the glucosidase II complex, which is responsible for the trimming of glucose residues from newly synthesized glycoproteins in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). This trimming is crucial for the proper folding of glycoproteins, as it facilitates the interaction of these proteins with chaperones that ensure correct folding and prevent misfolding or aggregation.

### Reaction Pathways
GANAB operates within the N-glycosylation pathway, specifically during the quality control phase of glycoprotein synthesis. As part of the glucosidase II complex, GANAB removes the terminal glucose residues from the N-linked oligosaccharides on glycoproteins. This step is essential for the glycoproteins to exit the ER and proceed through the secretory pathway. If the glycoproteins are not properly folded, they are retained in the ER, where they can be refolded or targeted for degradation through the ER-associated degradation (ERAD) pathway. The activity of GANAB ensures that only properly folded proteins advance through the secretory pathway, maintaining cellular function and homeostasis.

### Location
GANAB is primarily located in the endoplasmic reticulum, where it performs its role in glycoprotein processing. The enzyme’s presence in the ER is critical, as this is the site of glycoprotein synthesis and initial folding. By functioning within the ER, GANAB helps maintain the quality of proteins that are eventually transported to various cellular destinations or secreted outside the cell.

### Diseases
Mutations in the GANAB gene are associated with autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD), a genetic disorder characterized by the development of fluid-filled cysts in the kidneys and other organs. This condition is caused by defective protein processing due to the impaired function of GANAB, leading to abnormal cellular signaling and cyst formation. ADPKD is a progressive disease that can lead to kidney failure. Additionally, GANAB dysfunction has been implicated in other disorders related to protein misfolding and improper glycoprotein processing, underscoring the enzyme’s importance in cellular homeostasis and disease prevention.
