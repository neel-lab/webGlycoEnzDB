Human GNPTAB, which encodes for the alpha and beta subunits of GlcNAc-1-phosphotransferase, plays a critical role in the lysosomal enzyme targeting pathway.

Function
GlcNAc-1-phosphotransferase, the enzyme for which GNPTAB codes, is responsible for the initial step in the formation of the mannose 6-phosphate (Man-6-P) recognition marker on lysosomal enzymes. This marker is crucial for directing these enzymes to the lysosome. Without it, enzymes are misdirected to the extracellular space, leading to a deficiency of essential hydrolases in lysosomes.

Reaction Pathways
The GNPTAB-encoded enzyme functions in the post-translational modification of lysosomal enzymes. It catalyzes the transfer of GlcNAc-1-phosphate to specific mannose residues on the oligosaccharides of these enzymes, which is the first step in generating the Man-6-P marker. This marker is essential for the enzymes to be correctly trafficked to the lysosomes.

Location
GNPTAB is expressed in various tissues and cells, given its fundamental role in lysosomal function. The protein product, GlcNAc-1-phosphotransferase, is located in the Golgi apparatus where it carries out its catalytic function.

Related Diseases
Mutations in the GNPTAB gene are linked to mucolipidosis types II alpha/beta (ML II alpha/beta, OMIM: 252600) and III alpha/beta (ML III alpha/beta, OMIM: 252605). These are lysosomal storage disorders characterized by skeletal abnormalities, developmental delays, and other systemic symptoms. These diseases result from the failure of lysosomal enzymes to be correctly targeted to the lysosome, leading to their deficiency within this organelle and subsequent cellular dysfunction.
