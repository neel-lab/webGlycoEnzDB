### Function
Human PLOD2, or procollagen-lysine, 2-oxoglutarate 5-dioxygenase 2, is an enzyme crucial for the post-translational modification of collagen, specifically for the hydroxylation of lysine residues in collagen molecules. This hydroxylation process converts lysine into hydroxylysine, which is vital for the stability and proper function of collagen. Hydroxylysine residues are essential for the cross-linking of collagen fibers, which strengthens connective tissues and allows for the proper formation of the extracellular matrix.

### Reaction Pathways
PLOD2 operates within the collagen biosynthesis pathway, where it hydroxylates lysine residues within the collagen triple helix. This reaction requires molecular oxygen, iron, and 2-oxoglutarate as cofactors. The hydroxylated lysine residues are critical for the glycosylation and cross-linking processes that occur later in collagen maturation. These modifications are essential for the proper assembly and tensile strength of collagen fibers, which provide structural support to tissues such as skin, bones, tendons, and ligaments.

### Location
PLOD2 is localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), the site of collagen synthesis and post-translational modification. The ER provides an optimal environment for PLOD2 to hydroxylate lysine residues in procollagen, ensuring that the collagen molecules are correctly modified before being secreted and assembled into mature collagen fibers. PLOD2’s localization in the ER is crucial for its role in producing functional collagen that contributes to the strength and resilience of connective tissues.

### Diseases
Mutations in the PLOD2 gene are associated with a condition known as Bruck syndrome, specifically Bruck syndrome type 2. This rare genetic disorder is characterized by bone fragility, joint contractures, and scoliosis. The disease results from defects in lysyl hydroxylation, leading to improperly formed collagen that lacks the necessary cross-links for strength and stability. As a result, individuals with Bruck syndrome are prone to fractures and have weakened connective tissues. Understanding PLOD2’s function is critical for diagnosing Bruck syndrome and developing potential therapies to manage its symptoms.
