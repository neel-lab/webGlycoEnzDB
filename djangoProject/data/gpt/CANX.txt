### Function

Human CANX, also known as calnexin, is a calcium-binding chaperone protein primarily involved in the quality control of newly synthesized glycoproteins in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). It plays a crucial role in ensuring that these glycoproteins fold correctly and attain their proper conformation before being transported to their final destinations. Calnexin binds to monoglucosylated N-linked glycans on the nascent glycoproteins, preventing premature aggregation and assisting in the proper folding and assembly of these proteins.

### Reaction Pathways

CANX operates in the calnexin/calreticulin cycle, a key component of the ER's quality control system. This cycle involves the recognition and binding of newly synthesized glycoproteins that have been partially folded. Calnexin specifically interacts with these glycoproteins through their N-linked glycans, which have been modified by the addition of a single glucose residue. This binding retains the glycoproteins in the ER, allowing additional time for proper folding and preventing their transport to the Golgi apparatus if they are misfolded. The enzyme glucosidase II then removes the glucose residue, releasing the glycoprotein from calnexin. If the glycoprotein is correctly folded, it proceeds to the Golgi. If not, it is re-glucosylated by UDP-glucose:glycoprotein glucosyltransferase (UGGT) and rebinds to calnexin for further folding attempts.

### Location

Calnexin is predominantly located in the endoplasmic reticulum membrane. As a type I integral membrane protein, it spans the ER membrane with its N-terminal domain facing the ER lumen, where it interacts with glycoproteins, and its C-terminal domain extending into the cytoplasm. This strategic localization allows calnexin to perform its chaperone functions effectively within the ER, ensuring that only properly folded glycoproteins are transported to the Golgi apparatus for further processing and eventual secretion or membrane insertion.

### Diseases

Mutations or dysregulation of CANX are associated with several diseases. Calnexin deficiency can lead to the accumulation of misfolded proteins in the ER, contributing to ER stress and the activation of the unfolded protein response (UPR). This stress response is implicated in various conditions, including neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease, where protein misfolding and aggregation are central features. Additionally, calnexin mutations have been linked to certain congenital disorders of glycosylation (CDG), which are characterized by defects in glycoprotein folding and function. These disorders can present with a wide range of symptoms, including developmental delays, neurological impairment, and immunodeficiency.

In summary, CANX (calnexin) is an essential chaperone protein involved in the quality control of glycoprotein folding within the ER. Its function and proper regulation are critical for maintaining cellular homeostasis, and its dysregulation is associated with significant diseases.
