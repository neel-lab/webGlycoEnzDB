### Function
Human HS3ST3A1, or heparan sulfate 3-O-sulfotransferase 3A1, is an enzyme involved in the post-translational modification of heparan sulfate, a glycosaminoglycan that is critical for various cellular processes. HS3ST3A1 specifically catalyzes the addition of a sulfate group to the 3-O position of glucosamine residues within heparan sulfate chains. This sulfation event is essential for creating binding sites for numerous proteins, including antithrombin and chemokines, thereby influencing processes such as anticoagulation, cell signaling, and viral entry.

These interactions are critical in pathways related to blood coagulation, where 3-O-sulfated heparan sulfate enhances the activity of antithrombin, and in immune responses, where it modulates the activity of chemokines.

HS3ST3A1 is localized in the Golgi apparatus, where it participates in the fine-tuning of heparan sulfate proteoglycans. The Golgi apparatus is the cellular organelle responsible for the modification and sorting of proteins and lipids. Within this environment, HS3ST3A1 ensures that heparan sulfate chains are properly sulfated before they are exported to the cell surface or the extracellular matrix, where they engage in various biological functions.

Dysregulation or altered expression of HS3ST3A1 has been implicated in various diseases, particularly cancer and viral infections. In cancer, abnormal sulfation patterns due to HS3ST3A1 activity can affect tumor growth, invasion, and metastasis by altering the interaction of heparan sulfate with growth factors and other signaling molecules. Additionally, some viruses, such as herpes simplex virus, exploit 3-O-sulfated heparan sulfate for cell entry, making HS3ST3A1 a potential target for antiviral therapies.
