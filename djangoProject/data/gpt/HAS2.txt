Hyaluronan Synthase 2 (HAS2) is one of the key enzymes responsible for the synthesis of hyaluronan (HA) in human tissues. HAS2 plays a crucial role in numerous physiological and pathological processes, owing to HA's involvement in cell signaling, tissue repair, and inflammation.

Functionally, HAS2 catalyzes the formation of HA by adding repeating disaccharide units of glucuronic acid and N-acetylglucosamine to the growing HA polymer. This enzymatic process is essential for the production of HA, which provides structural integrity to tissues, facilitates cell migration, and is involved in processes like wound healing and tissue hydration. HA synthesized by HAS2 is known for its particularly large size, significantly affecting its biological functions and interactions in the extracellular matrix.

HAS2, alongside HAS1 and HAS3, differs in enzymatic properties such as stability, substrate kinetics, and the rate of sugar chain elongation. This diversity allows the synthesis of distinct forms of HA, tailored for specific physiological roles. The specific characteristics of HA produced by HAS2 make it particularly important in developmental processes and tissue organization.

Located primarily on the plasma membrane, HAS2 is strategically positioned to synthesize and secrete HA directly into the extracellular space. This localization is crucial for its role in modulating the extracellular environment of cells and tissues.

Disorders associated with dysregulated HA synthesis, potentially involving HAS2, include developmental abnormalities, cancer, and inflammatory diseases. For example, HAS2's role in embryogenesis is highlighted by the embryonic lethality observed in Has2-null mice, indicating its critical involvement in early developmental stages.
