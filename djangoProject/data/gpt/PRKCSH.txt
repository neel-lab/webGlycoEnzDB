Human PRKCSH (Protein Kinase C Substrate 80K-H), also known as hepatocystin, is a crucial protein involved in the regulation of protein quality control within the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). It serves as a regulatory subunit for glucosidase II, an enzyme essential for the proper folding and processing of glycoproteins in the ER.

PRKCSH is predominantly located in the ER, where it plays a key role in the glycoprotein processing pathway. Glucosidase II, to which PRKCSH binds, is responsible for trimming glucose residues from nascent glycoproteins, a process vital for their correct folding and maturation. This trimming mechanism is part of the quality control system that ensures only properly folded proteins are transported from the ER to their destination within the cell.

Mutations in the PRKCSH gene have been linked to autosomal dominant polycystic liver disease (PCLD). PCLD is characterized by the development of multiple fluid-filled cysts in the liver, leading to hepatomegaly (enlarged liver) and associated complications. The precise mechanism by which PRKCSH mutations lead to cyst formation is not fully understood but is believed to involve disruptions in protein processing and ER homeostasis, impacting epithelial cell function in the liver.
