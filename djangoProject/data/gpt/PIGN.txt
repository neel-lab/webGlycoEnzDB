### Function
Human PIGN, or phosphatidylinositol glycan anchor biosynthesis class N, is an enzyme crucial for the biosynthesis of glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchors. GPI anchors are glycolipid structures that attach specific proteins to the cell membrane, where they play vital roles in cell signaling, adhesion, and immune response. PIGN is responsible for transferring ethanolamine phosphate to the second mannose in the GPI precursor, an essential step in the proper assembly and maturation of the GPI anchor.

### Reaction Pathways
PIGN functions within the GPI anchor biosynthesis pathway, which takes place in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). After the initial mannose residues have been added to the GPI precursor, PIGN catalyzes the transfer of ethanolamine phosphate to the second mannose. This modification is crucial for the structural integrity and function of the GPI anchor, allowing it to effectively tether proteins to the cell membrane. The correct functioning of PIGN ensures the successful progression of the GPI anchor assembly, which is essential for the proper localization and function of GPI-anchored proteins.

### Location
PIGN is localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where it performs its role in the GPI anchor biosynthesis pathway. The ER provides the necessary environment for PIGN to interact with other enzymes involved in GPI anchor formation, facilitating the proper modification and assembly of GPI anchors. The enzyme's presence in the ER ensures that GPI-anchored proteins are correctly processed before they are transported to the cell surface.

### Diseases
Mutations in the PIGN gene are associated with inherited GPI deficiencies (IGDs), particularly a condition known as multiple congenital anomalies-hypotonia-seizures syndrome 1 (MCAHS1). This rare genetic disorder is characterized by developmental delays, intellectual disability, seizures, and various congenital anomalies. The disorder results from defective GPI anchor biosynthesis due to impaired PIGN activity, leading to the mislocalization and dysfunction of GPI-anchored proteins. Understanding PIGN's role is crucial for diagnosing and developing treatments for conditions associated with GPI anchor deficiencies.
