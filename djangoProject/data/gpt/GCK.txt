Human GCK, or glucokinase, is an enzyme predominantly found in the liver and pancreas, playing a key role in glucose metabolism.

Function
GCK functions as a glucose sensor, especially in the pancreas. It catalyzes the phosphorylation of glucose to glucose-6-phosphate (G6P), the first step in glycolysis and glycogenesis. In the liver, GCK helps regulate glucose storage and release, while in the pancreas, it is involved in the regulation of insulin secretion.

Reaction Pathways
GCK participates in two major metabolic pathways: glycolysis and glycogenesis. In glycolysis, GCK’s action converts glucose to G6P, which then undergoes a series of reactions to produce pyruvate, generating ATP in the process. In glycogenesis, G6P is converted into glycogen for storage. GCK's activity is critical in maintaining blood glucose levels within a narrow range, particularly after eating.

Location
GCK is primarily located in the liver and beta cells of the pancreas. In the liver, it plays a role in glucose storage and release, while in the pancreas, it is crucial for insulin release in response to high blood glucose levels.

Related Diseases
Mutations in the GCK gene can lead to several forms of diabetes. Heterozygous inactivating mutations cause a mild form of diabetes known as MODY2 (Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young type 2). Conversely, activating mutations in GCK result in persistent hyperinsulinemic hypoglycemia of infancy (PHHI), a condition characterized by excessive insulin secretion leading to low blood sugar.
