### Function

Human FKRP (fukutin-related protein) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in the glycosylation of alpha-dystroglycan (α-DG), a glycoprotein involved in maintaining the integrity of muscle tissue. FKRP is responsible for adding ribitol-5-phosphate to the glycan structures on α-DG, a modification necessary for the proper binding of α-DG to extracellular matrix proteins such as laminin. This glycosylation process is vital for stabilizing the muscle cell membrane during contraction and relaxation.

### Reaction Pathways

FKRP functions in a specific glycosylation pathway that modifies O-mannosylated glycans on α-DG. This pathway begins with the phosphorylation of mannose residues, followed by the addition of ribitol-5-phosphate by FKRP. This modification is critical for the subsequent addition of xylose and glucuronic acid, which are needed to form a functional binding site on α-DG for extracellular matrix components. The pathway is complex, involving multiple enzymes and steps, with FKRP being central to the post-phosphoryl modification necessary for the maturation of functional α-DG.

### Location

FKRP is localized primarily in the Golgi apparatus, where it interacts with other glycosylation enzymes. The Golgi is the site of protein modification and trafficking, providing the necessary environment for FKRP to perform its function in glycan synthesis. The localization of FKRP within the Golgi is essential for its role in the precise and sequential addition of sugars to α-DG, ensuring that the glycoprotein is correctly modified before it is transported to the cell surface.

### Diseases

Mutations in the FKRP gene are associated with several forms of muscular dystrophy, including congenital muscular dystrophy type 1C (MDC1C) and limb-girdle muscular dystrophy type 2I (LGMD2I). These conditions, collectively known as dystroglycanopathies, are characterized by the improper glycosylation of α-DG, leading to muscle weakness, degeneration, and in severe cases, brain and eye abnormalities.
