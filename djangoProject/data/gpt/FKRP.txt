Human FKRP (Fukutin-Related Protein) is a significant enzyme involved in the post-translational modification of dystroglycan, a crucial component in muscle tissue. FKRP plays a vital role in the glycosylation process of dystroglycan, which is essential for maintaining the stability and integrity of muscle fibers.

Function and Reaction Pathways:
FKRP is involved in the addition of a ribitol-phosphate moiety to a specific glycan (sugar chain) on dystroglycan. This modification is a part of a larger glycosylation process that is crucial for dystroglycan to effectively bind to laminin in the extracellular matrix. The binding of dystroglycan to laminin is essential for the structural integrity and function of muscle fibers, as it links the internal cytoskeleton of muscle cells to the extracellular matrix, providing strength and resilience to muscle tissue.

Location:
FKRP is primarily located in the Golgi apparatus, where it participates in the glycosylation of dystroglycan. This localization is crucial for its role in modifying dystroglycan as it is processed in the secretory pathway of the cell.

Diseases:
Mutations in the FKRP gene are associated with a spectrum of muscular dystrophies, including Limb-Girdle Muscular Dystrophy type 2I (LGMD2I), Congenital Muscular Dystrophy type 1C (MDC1C), and Fukuyama Congenital Muscular Dystrophy. These conditions are characterized by muscle weakness and degeneration due to the impaired function of glycosylated dystroglycan. The severity and symptoms of these diseases can vary, ranging from mild muscle weakness to severe congenital forms that affect motor development and can be life-threatening.
