### Function
Human GBA2, or non-lysosomal glucosylceramidase, is an enzyme that plays a critical role in the metabolism of glycolipids. Unlike GBA1, which operates in lysosomes, GBA2 catalyzes the hydrolysis of glucosylceramide into glucose and ceramide in non-lysosomal compartments. This enzymatic activity is important for maintaining the balance of glucosylceramide and ceramide levels in cells, contributing to various cellular processes, including membrane structure and signaling.

### Reaction Pathways
GBA2 functions within the sphingolipid metabolism pathway. It breaks down glucosylceramide, a glycosphingolipid, into glucose and ceramide. This reaction is crucial for the regulation of cellular sphingolipid content, impacting membrane dynamics, cell signaling, and apoptosis. The ceramide produced by GBA2’s action can be further utilized in the synthesis of other complex sphingolipids or can act as a signaling molecule itself, influencing processes such as cell growth, differentiation, and death.

### Location
GBA2 is localized in non-lysosomal compartments of the cell, particularly in the endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi apparatus. These locations are key sites for lipid processing and transport, allowing GBA2 to effectively regulate the levels of glucosylceramide and ceramide in the cell. The enzyme’s presence in these organelles highlights its role in the broader sphingolipid metabolism that occurs outside of the lysosome.

### Diseases
Mutations in the GBA2 gene have been associated with spastic paraplegia type 46 (SPG46), a neurological disorder characterized by progressive weakness and stiffness of the lower limbs. This condition is linked to the accumulation of glucosylceramide due to impaired GBA2 activity, leading to disruptions in cellular lipid homeostasis and neuronal function. Additionally, GBA2 dysfunction has been implicated in other disorders, such as cerebellar ataxia, where patients experience coordination and balance difficulties. The exact mechanisms by which GBA2 mutations contribute to these diseases are still being researched, but it is clear that the enzyme plays a vital role in maintaining normal cellular and neurological function.
