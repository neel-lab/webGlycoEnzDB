### Function
Human PIGA, or phosphatidylinositol glycan anchor biosynthesis class A, is an enzyme crucial for the initial step in the biosynthesis of glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchors. GPI anchors are glycolipid structures that tether certain proteins to the cell membrane, playing essential roles in various cellular processes, including signal transduction, cell adhesion, and immune response. PIGA catalyzes the transfer of N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) to phosphatidylinositol, the first step in assembling the GPI anchor.

### Reaction Pathways
PIGA operates in the GPI anchor biosynthesis pathway, a multi-step process that begins with the action of PIGA in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). PIGA transfers GlcNAc to phosphatidylinositol, forming GlcNAc-phosphatidylinositol. This molecule undergoes a series of modifications, including deacetylation, acylation, and the addition of mannose residues, ultimately resulting in a complete GPI anchor. The GPI anchor is then attached to proteins, facilitating their localization to the cell surface where they perform their functions.

### Location
PIGA is localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), the site of GPI anchor assembly. The ER provides the environment necessary for the initial and subsequent steps of GPI anchor biosynthesis, ensuring that proteins destined for the cell surface are properly anchored. The ER localization of PIGA is crucial for its role in the early stages of GPI anchor formation, setting the foundation for the proper functioning of GPI-anchored proteins.

### Diseases
Mutations in the PIGA gene can lead to a group of disorders known as glycosylphosphatidylinositol biosynthesis defects, the most well-known of which is paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH). PNH is a rare acquired hematological disorder characterized by the destruction of red blood cells, blood clots, and bone marrow failure. The condition arises because of somatic mutations in PIGA, leading to the absence or dysfunction of GPI-anchored proteins on blood cells, making them susceptible to immune attack. Additionally, PIGA mutations are associated with rare congenital disorders marked by developmental delays, seizures, and other systemic abnormalities.
