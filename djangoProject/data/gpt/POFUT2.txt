### Function
Human POFUT2, or protein O-fucosyltransferase 2, is an enzyme that adds fucose, a sugar molecule, to specific serine or threonine residues on target proteins, particularly those with thrombospondin type 1 repeats (TSRs). This process, known as O-fucosylation, is crucial for the proper folding, stability, and function of these proteins. POFUT2's activity is essential for the maturation and function of various proteins involved in cell signaling, adhesion, and extracellular matrix organization.

### Reaction Pathways
POFUT2 operates within the fucosylation pathway, where it catalyzes the transfer of fucose from GDP-fucose, the donor molecule, to the hydroxyl group of serine or threonine residues within the TSRs of target proteins. This O-fucosylation modification is vital for the correct folding and secretion of these proteins. Unlike POFUT1, which targets the Notch receptor, POFUT2 specifically modifies TSR-containing proteins, which play roles in processes like angiogenesis, cell migration, and tissue repair.

### Location
POFUT2 is localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), where it functions during the early stages of protein synthesis and folding. The ER provides the necessary environment for POFUT2 to interact with nascent TSR-containing proteins, ensuring they are correctly modified before being transported to the Golgi apparatus for further processing and eventual secretion. The localization of POFUT2 in the ER is essential for its role in maintaining the proper function and stability of TSR-containing proteins.

### Diseases
Mutations or dysregulation of the POFUT2 gene can lead to developmental disorders and diseases related to extracellular matrix dysfunction. Abnormal POFUT2 activity has been implicated in conditions such as Peters plus syndrome, a rare congenital disorder characterized by eye abnormalities, short stature, and other developmental defects. The improper O-fucosylation of TSR-containing proteins due to POFUT2 dysfunction can disrupt normal cellular functions, leading to these congenital anomalies. Understanding POFUT2's role in protein maturation and disease pathogenesis is crucial for developing therapeutic strategies to manage these conditions.
