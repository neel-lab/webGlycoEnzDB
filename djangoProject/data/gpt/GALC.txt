### Function

Human GALC (galactosylceramidase) is a lysosomal enzyme essential for the degradation of galactolipids, particularly galactosylceramide and psychosine (galactosylsphingosine). These lipids are major components of myelin, the insulating sheath surrounding nerve fibers. GALC hydrolyzes the galactose moiety from these molecules, a critical step in the turnover and recycling of myelin. This process is vital for maintaining the health and function of the nervous system.

### Reaction Pathways

GALC operates within the lysosomal degradation pathway, where it hydrolyzes galactosylceramide into ceramide and galactose. This pathway is crucial for the normal breakdown and recycling of complex lipids in the nervous system. If GALC activity is deficient, its substrates accumulate, leading to the disruption of cellular functions. Psychosine accumulation, in particular, is toxic to oligodendrocytes, the cells responsible for myelin production, which can lead to widespread demyelination.

### Diseases

Deficiency in GALC activity leads to Krabbe disease, a severe lysosomal storage disorder. Krabbe disease is characterized by the accumulation of galactosylceramide and psychosine, which causes progressive damage to the nervous system, resulting in demyelination, severe neurological impairment, and often death in early childhood. The disease is inherited in an autosomal recessive manner, and currently, the primary treatment is hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, although it is only partially effective and must be administered early in the disease course.
