GALC is a lysosomal enzyme that plays a vital role in the metabolism of galactolipids in the myelin sheath. It catalyzes the hydrolysis of galactosylceramide (GalCer) to ceramide and galactose, as well as the breakdown of psychosine (galactosylsphingosine), a cytotoxic compound that can accumulate in nerve cells.

GALC is predominantly expressed in the central and peripheral nervous systems, especially in the myelin-producing cells - oligodendrocytes in the CNS and Schwann cells in the PNS. It is critical for maintaining the health and integrity of myelin, the insulating sheath around nerve fibers, which is essential for the rapid transmission of nerve impulses.

Mutations in the GALC gene are associated with Krabbe disease (globoid cell leukodystrophy), a rare and often severe lysosomal storage disorder. Krabbe disease is characterized by the accumulation of psychosine, leading to the destruction of myelin, progressive neurodegeneration, and, in severe cases, early childhood death. Symptoms include irritability, muscle weakness, feeding difficulties, developmental delays, and severe motor and cognitive impairment. Currently, there is no cure for Krabbe disease, and treatment focuses on symptom management and supportive care.
