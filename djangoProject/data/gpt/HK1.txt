Hexokinase 1 (HK1) is a key enzyme in the glycolytic pathway, crucial for the metabolism of glucose in human cells. It catalyzes the first step in glycolysis, the conversion of glucose to glucose-6-phosphate (G6P), a process fundamental to cellular energy production.

Functionally, HK1 binds to glucose and ATP, transferring a phosphate group from ATP to glucose to form G6P. This initial step is vital as it traps glucose within the cell and makes it available for further metabolic processes. HK1's activity is highly regulated, as it sets the pace for the entire glycolytic pathway, which is a primary source of energy production, especially in conditions of limited oxygen.

HK1 is predominantly found in tissues with high ATP demands, such as the brain, skeletal muscle, and red blood cells. In these tissues, efficient glucose utilization and energy production are critical for proper functioning. HK1 is bound to the outer mitochondrial membrane, which allows for efficient coupling of glycolysis with oxidative phosphorylation in the mitochondria for ATP production.

Dysfunction in HK1 can lead to various diseases, most notably affecting energy metabolism. Mutations in the HK1 gene have been associated with a form of nonspherocytic hemolytic anemia, a condition where red blood cells break down prematurely, leading to hemolysis. This is due to the critical role of HK1 in red blood cells, where it facilitates the sole energy-producing pathway available in these cells.
