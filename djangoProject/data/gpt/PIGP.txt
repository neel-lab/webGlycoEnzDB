GPI anchors are essential glycolipids that tether a variety of proteins to the cell surface, facilitating key biological functions including signal transduction, cell adhesion, and immune response.

PIGP is involved in an early step of GPI anchor synthesis, where it participates in the transfer of N-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) to phosphatidylinositol (PI). This enzymatic action is crucial for the initial formation of the GPI anchor, setting the foundation for subsequent modifications that result in the complete, functional GPI anchor necessary for protein attachment to the cell membrane.

Located in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), PIGP is part of the complex process that constructs GPI anchors. The proper assembly of GPI anchors is essential for the correct localization and functioning of various cell surface proteins, including enzymes, receptors, and adhesion molecules.

While specific diseases directly linked to PIGP dysfunction are not extensively documented, defects in the GPI anchor biosynthesis process can lead to a group of rare genetic disorders known as GPI biosynthesis defects (GPIBDs). These disorders may present with a range of symptoms, such as developmental delays, intellectual disabilities, and seizures, reflecting the critical role of GPI-anchored proteins in various physiological processes, particularly in neural development.
