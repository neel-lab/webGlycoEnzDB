Human GUSB, or Beta-glucuronidase, is a lysosomal enzyme integral to the degradation of glycosaminoglycans (GAGs).

Function
GUSB catalyzes the hydrolysis of glucuronic acid residues from the non-reducing ends of GAGs like dermatan sulfate and chondroitin sulfate. This enzymatic action is crucial for the stepwise degradation of GAGs, enabling their subsequent recycling or excretion. The efficient breakdown of GAGs is essential for maintaining cellular homeostasis and preventing their pathological accumulation.

Reaction Pathways
GUSB is involved in the lysosomal catabolic pathway of GAGs. In this pathway, GUSB acts by removing glucuronic acid residues from GAGs, facilitating their breakdown into simpler molecules. This process is vital for the regular degradation and recycling of GAGs, which are significant components of the extracellular matrix and are involved in various cellular processes.

Location
GUSB is located in the lysosomes, cellular organelles equipped with enzymes necessary for breaking down various types of biomolecules. Lysosomes serve as the primary site for the degradation of complex molecules like GAGs, which are delivered to them for processing.

Related Diseases
Deficiency in GUSB activity leads to a lysosomal storage disease known as Mucopolysaccharidosis Type VII (MPS VII), also called Sly syndrome. MPS VII is characterized by the accumulation of GAGs in tissues and organs, leading to various symptoms including skeletal deformities, developmental delays, and, in severe cases, neurological impairment. The severity and range of symptoms in MPS VII can vary among affected individuals.
