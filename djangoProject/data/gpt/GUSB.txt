### Function
Human GUSB, or beta-glucuronidase, is an enzyme that plays a vital role in the degradation of glycosaminoglycans (GAGs). GUSB catalyzes the hydrolysis of glucuronic acid residues from the non-reducing ends of GAGs, such as heparan sulfate, dermatan sulfate, and chondroitin sulfate. This enzymatic activity is crucial for the proper breakdown and recycling of GAGs within lysosomes, ensuring cellular and tissue homeostasis.

### Reaction Pathways
GUSB functions within the lysosomal degradation pathway, specifically targeting GAGs for breakdown. The enzyme cleaves glucuronic acid residues from the carbohydrate chains of GAGs, a necessary step in the sequential degradation of these complex molecules. The proper function of GUSB is essential for preventing the accumulation of partially degraded GAGs, which could otherwise disrupt cellular function and lead to various diseases.

MPS VII is characterized by the accumulation of undegraded GAGs within lysosomes, leading to a wide range of symptoms, including skeletal abnormalities, cognitive impairment, organomegaly, and heart issues. The severity of the disease varies, but it can be life-threatening if left untreated. Enzyme replacement therapy and other treatments are being explored to manage the symptoms of MPS VII and improve the quality of life for affected individuals.
